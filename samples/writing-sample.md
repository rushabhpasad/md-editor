# The Art of Writing

Writing is thinking made visible. When we put words on a page — or screen — we externalize our thoughts and give them shape. A good Markdown editor should disappear, leaving only you and your ideas.

---

## Why Plain Text Matters

In an age of rich-text editors and word processors, there is something profoundly freeing about plain text. Plain text is:

- **Portable** — it works everywhere, forever
- **Fast** — no loading times, no lag
- **Focused** — no distracting formatting options
- **Future-proof** — text files from 1970 still open perfectly today

> "The best tool is the one that gets out of your way."

Markdown extends plain text with just enough syntax to express structure — headings, emphasis, lists, links — without sacrificing simplicity.

## A Writer's Workflow

### Morning Pages

Start with **free writing**. No editing, no formatting. Just words. Three pages, every morning, no exceptions. Anne Lamott calls this "shitty first drafts" — and she's right. The first draft is for you. The second draft is for your reader.

### The Editing Pass

Once the first draft is down, let it breathe. Come back with fresh eyes. Ask yourself:

1. What is this piece *really* about?
2. Does every paragraph serve that purpose?
3. Is there anything here that could be cut?

The goal is clarity. Every word should earn its place.

### The Final Polish

Read your work aloud. Your ear catches what your eye misses. If you stumble over a phrase, your reader will too. Simplify. Shorten. Cut.

## On Distraction

The single greatest enemy of writing is distraction. A split-pane editor like MD Editor gives you a choice: work in **Edit mode** when you need to type, switch to **Preview mode** when you need to read. The toggle is always one click away.

*"A writer only begins a book. A reader finishes it."*
— Samuel Johnson
